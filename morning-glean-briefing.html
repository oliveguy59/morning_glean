The story

What we built — and why it matters

Most news readers are designed to keep you reading. More clicks, more time, more engagement. The algorithms behind them learn what makes you react — outrage, fear, tribal validation — and serve you more of it. This is not accidental. It is the business model.

We set out to build something different: a daily morning briefing that spans the full ideological spectrum, labels its own biases openly, and is designed to help you notice those biases rather than be captured by them. We called it the Morning Glean.

The goal is not to tell you what to think. The goal is to show you the whole field, mark where the mines are, and let you walk it yourself. — The design principle behind Morning Glean

Here is a tour of every design decision we made — what the research said, what we chose, and why.

Design decision 1

The Spectrum: Five Positions, Measured in Standard Deviations

Most "balanced" news compares two positions: left and right. But the actual landscape of political opinion is a distribution — a bell curve. Two positions ignores where most of the diversity actually lives.

We mapped five positions, each roughly one standard deviation from center along the distribution of American political opinion:

Center. Consensus-seeking, establishment, data-driven.

Far right · Hard money. Libertarian, gold, sovereignty, fringe.
Why we removed the + and − signs In an early version, positions were labeled −2, −1, 0, +1, +2. The math implied that "left" is negative and "right" is positive. That is a value judgment embedded invisibly in a label. We replaced them with L2/L1/C/R1/R2 — direction and distance only. No score.

Crucially, each day's five topics are chosen from what each community is actually resonating with — not from the mainstream headline rotation. A far-right audience today may be talking about petrodollar collapse while mainstream media leads with the same three stories everywhere. The Glean captures that divergence.

Design decision 2

Topic Selection: What Each Community Is Actually Talking About

The hardest part of the morning process is not writing — it is listening. To produce five genuinely distinct topics, we search inside each ideological community's own publications, not from a shared news feed.

This is harder than it sounds. Most "multi-perspective" tools take one story and describe it from five angles. We take five different stories — each the genuine priority of a different community in the past 15 hours — and present them side by side.

60%

The 60% Distinctness Rule

Topics must be at least 60% distinct from each other. If far-right and center-right communities are both leading with the same story, we find a more distinctive R2 topic. The point is breadth of the information landscape, not variations on a single theme.

The scope is the "American Empire" — anywhere the United States is generating significant news or consequence, including foreign policy, trade, military presence, financial influence, and cultural reach.

Design decision 3

Reading Layout: Center-Out, Not Left-to-Right

Where you place things shapes how people interpret them. We tested two reading orders: chronological left-to-right (L2 → R2) and center-out (C first, then L1/R1 side by side, then L2/R2 side by side).

Start here — most grounded material
C — Center
One step out — symmetric pair
L1 — Center Left
R1 — Center Right
Two steps out — furthest pair
L2 — Far Left
R2 — Far Right

Center-out has two psychological advantages. First, you begin from the most consensus-grounded material and move outward — you are calibrated before you meet the extremes. Second, you always see the left and right mirror of each distance simultaneously, preventing either side from becoming the "normal" against which the other is judged strange.

Design decision 4

The Color Scale: ROYGBIV as a Propaganda Filter

Every news source has a quality rating — not for politics, but for epistemic quality: the ratio of evidence and reasoning to rhetoric and emotional manipulation. We mapped that to the most intuitive color gradient humans know: the rainbow.

Notice that the scale does not reach Violet in practice — Violet is reserved for scripture, which is a category of truth-claim entirely outside journalism. The best a news source can achieve is Indigo: rigorous, well-sourced, honest about uncertainty.

The worst-actor rule The color assigned to a topic in the navigation bar is not the average quality of its sources — it is the worst. If one of five sources covering a topic is outright propaganda, the topic displays red. This prevents averaging from hiding the worst influence in the mix.

Each individual source also carries a color-coded left border the full height of its card, so your eye can scan the quality of a source at a glance before reading a word.

Design decision 5

Navigation: The Masthead Model

We had a design choice: should the full spectrum navigation bar stay permanently visible as you read, or disappear when you scroll down into content?

This turns out to be a research question about anchoring bias and framing effects. When context disappears, readers evaluate each source in isolation — and the source's own framing dominates. A sticky spectrum bar would fight that drift, but a full-width bar dominating the screen while you read is visually intrusive and changes the reading experience.

The solution: the newspaper masthead model

A physical newspaper shows you its full masthead and table of contents when you pick it up. Once you turn to page 6, the masthead is gone — but you hold the whole paper in your hand. The physical weight and structure remind you of the whole even while you read a part.

We reproduced this: the full spectrum TOC is visible at the top of the page. As you scroll, it collapses to a slim mini-navigation bar showing just the topic names and positions. The 5-pixel rainbow stripe remains fixed at the very top of every screen as a constant, unobtrusive reminder that you are reading inside a spectrum.

Design decision 6

Swappable Design: Every Visual Decision Is a Token

Good design is provisional. A daughter might read this and say "the colors are too harsh" or "the font feels clinical." We built the visual design so any of it can be changed in one place.

Every color, every spacing value, every font, every size — all defined as CSS custom properties (design tokens) at the top of the stylesheet. To change the entire color palette, you edit seven lines. To change the typography, you change two. Nothing is hardcoded in the body of the design.

Good systems design separates what the design is from where the design is applied. Tokens are the "what." Styles are the "where." — Design systems principle

What comes next

Three Ideas for Where This Could Go

The Morning Glean is a daily news reader. But the underlying structure — spectrum-positioned perspectives, propaganda-quality scoring, center-out reading layout — is a framework that could be applied to many things. Here are three directions worth considering.

1. The Editorial Glean — Opinion, Not News

News reports what happened. Editorials argue what it means. The same five-position spectrum applied to editorial and opinion writing would show you not just the events but the competing interpretations of those events. Substack in particular has an extraordinary range of independent voices across the spectrum — many of them doing original thinking that never appears in mainstream outlets.

The propaganda filter becomes even more interesting here: opinion pieces are supposed to advocate. The question becomes whether the advocacy is honest about being advocacy, whether it engages the strongest counterarguments, and whether its factual claims are sourced.

Substack Opinion Editorials Independent voices

2. The Empire Glean — Other Nations, Their Own Spectrum

The Morning Glean currently covers the American Empire — the world as seen from and through American influence. But every nation has its own internal spectrum. What does the five-position spectrum look like inside Brazil, or Germany, or India? What are their far-left and far-right communities saying? What stories are they leading with that never reach American media at all?

Running the Glean for a second nation and comparing the two side by side would reveal not just ideological diversity within a country but the degree to which different nations are living in different information realities about the same world.

Global perspectives Comparative media Brazil EU India

3. The Historical Glean — Any Date, Any Era

The spectrum structure is not unique to today. Any significant historical moment — the 1968 election, the moon landing, the fall of the Berlin Wall, September 11 — had its own five-position spectrum of interpretation. With access to digitized historical news archives, the same framework could be applied to the past: what was the far-left saying the morning after the 1929 crash? What was the far-right saying the week before Pearl Harbor?

This turns Morning Glean into a tool not just for present-day media literacy but for historical empathy — understanding why people at a given moment saw the world the way they did, from inside their own information environment.

A closing thought

Why This Matters More Than It Looks

The Morning Glean is, on the surface, a news reader. But the deeper project is about something the research literature calls epistemic autonomy — the capacity to form your own beliefs through your own reasoning, rather than having them formed for you by the information environment you happen to inhabit.

The media landscape is not neutral. Every platform, every algorithm, every editorial judgment reflects a set of priorities. Most of those priorities are not yours. Morning Glean is an attempt to hand some of that control back: to see the whole spectrum before choosing where to look, to know the quality of a source before trusting it, and to read the far positions not to be converted but to understand.

If it helps even one person think more clearly about one story, it will have been worth the morning.

Be well-informed. Be hard to fool. Love widely. — Dad
